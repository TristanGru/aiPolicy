I. POLICY RECOMMENDATION
─────────────────────────
  • Establish the AI Accountability Oversight Board (AIAOB)
  • Independent federal body modeled on the PCAOB (Public Company Accounting Oversight Board)
  • Mandate rolling audits of high-risk deployed AI systems
  • Certify third-party AI auditing firms

  What to write: One clear, declarative paragraph. Define "rolling audits" on first
  use — continuous, change-triggered oversight with persistent data access, not
  one-time compliance snapshots. Name the domains in scope: healthcare, criminal
  justice, financial services, hiring, and critical infrastructure. The reader
  should finish this paragraph knowing exactly what the policy does and why it's
  different from existing proposals.

II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT  (~0.5 pages)
─────────────────────────────────────

  A. The State Patchwork Is Failing
     • CA SB-53 (Sep 2025), NY RAISE Act (Dec 2025), NYC Local Law 144, CO SB 21-169
     • All incompatible — different scopes, standards, and targets
     • Dec 2025 Executive Order is already preempting state laws
     • Preemption without a replacement standard leaves deployed AI ungoverned

  B. Audits Without Certified Auditors Fail (Your Exhibit A)
     • NYC Local Law 144 mandated bias audits for AI hiring tools
     • Only 18 of 391 covered employers (4.6%) posted compliant audit reports [1]
     • NY State Comptroller (Dec 2025): enforcement "currently ineffective" [2]
     • Root cause: companies picked their own auditors; auditors lacked technical expertise
     • The lesson: a bad audit mandate creates false safety, not real accountability

  C. The Deployment Gap No One Is Closing
     • SB-53 and RAISE Act regulate frontier model developers before deployment [3][4]
     • Nothing governs what happens after the model ships
     • Model drift — performance degrading as real-world data diverges from training data —
       is "the most common and least visible risk" of deployed AI systems [5]
     • A hiring algorithm fair at launch can drift into bias by month six

  D. The Enron Parallel
     • Before Sarbanes-Oxley (2002), companies chose their own financial auditors
     • Arthur Andersen audited Enron — the conflict of interest was structural, not personal
     • Congress created PCAOB: independent board, certified auditors, no direct
       company-to-auditor payment
     • The same structural failure — self-selected, uncredentialed auditors — exists in AI today

  What to write: Don't treat these as four separate sections. Weave them into two or
  three tight paragraphs that build a single argument: the current approach (fragmented
  state laws, voluntary frameworks, self-selected auditors) has already demonstrably
  failed, and the structural fix is known. The NYC LL144 stat (4.6% compliance) and
  the Enron parallel are your two strongest rhetorical weapons — use both.

III. IMPLEMENTATION  (~0.75 pages)
────────────────────────────────────

  A. The Board
     • Independent, non-partisan; housed under Commerce Department
     • Self-funded via registration fees from audited companies (no appropriations needed)
     • Sets national AI auditing standards
     • Certifies third-party auditing firms
     • Reports to Senate Commerce and House Energy & Commerce committees

  B. Certified Auditor Requirements
     • Core credential: technical competence (ML, statistics, model evaluation) +
       legal/compliance knowledge (anti-discrimination law, HIPAA, FCRA)
     • Auditing firms must employ domain experts on staff per sector audited
       (e.g., a healthcare AI auditor knows the algorithm AND the care pathway)
     • Annual certification renewal with continuing education
     • AIAOB assigns firms from a certified pool — companies pay into a federal audit
       fund, not to auditors directly (eliminates the Local Law 144 conflict of interest)

  C. Audit Trigger (Bright-Line Rule)
     Mandatory audit if the AI system meets ANY of:
     • Makes binding decisions about individuals (denied loan, rejected application,
       criminal risk score)
     • Operates in a named high-risk sector (healthcare, criminal justice, hiring,
       credit, housing, critical infrastructure)
     • Exceeds scale thresholds (>10,000 consequential decisions/year OR
       >50,000 users in a regulated domain)

     Open source rule: Deployers are always responsible in high-risk contexts.
     Model creators (e.g., Meta releasing Llama) are only covered if they specifically
     develop or market models for high-risk domains.

  D. The Rolling Audit Framework (Dual Trigger)
     Change-triggered:
     • Any model update, retraining, or new deployment context requires a new audit
     • Closes the gap left by frameworks that certify once and never revisit

     Time-tiered by risk:
     • Tier 1 — Critical (healthcare diagnosis, criminal sentencing, autonomous systems):
       quarterly audits + continuous monitoring access
     • Tier 2 — High (hiring, lending, insurance underwriting): semi-annual audits
     • Tier 3 — Elevated (content recommendation, educational assessment): annual audits
     • If a system qualifies for multiple tiers, the highest tier applies

     Audits check: performance drift, bias emergence, data integrity, alignment with
     stated purpose, incident response capability

  E. Enforcement
     • Graduated ladder: warning → mandatory remediation plan → fines (scaled to
       company revenue) → deployment suspension
     • Whistleblower protections for auditors and employees
     • Public disclosure of audit summaries (transparency without exposing trade secrets)

  What to write: This is the densest section — keep each point to one sentence.
  Lead with the board structure (it's the institutional anchor), then auditor
  qualifications (the originality claim), then the trigger rules (the bright line),
  then the rolling mechanism (the core differentiator). Enforcement can be one
  sentence if you're running long. Target 375 words maximum for this entire section.

IV. IMPACTS AND TRADE-OFFS  (~0.5 pages)
──────────────────────────────────────────

  Benefits:
  • Closes the deployment gap — catches model drift and post-launch failures that
    pre-deployment frameworks (SB-53, RAISE Act) cannot detect
  • Replaces the state patchwork with one national standard, solving the fragmentation
    the December 2025 EO creates without addressing [6]
  • Accountability without prohibition — failed audit triggers remediation, not a ban
  • Market-creating: certified AI auditor becomes a new professional class, like CPAs
    after SOX; creates industry incentive to support the standard

  Trade-offs:
  • Compliance costs: real, but offset by reduced litigation exposure — a clean audit
    record is a defensible evidentiary shield
  • Ramp-up time: 12-18 months to build AIAOB and certify initial auditor pool;
    phase in Tier 1 first, expand to Tiers 2 and 3 as capacity grows
  • Regulatory capture risk: mitigated by fee-based independence, rotating board
    membership, and mandatory public disclosure
  • Scope: does NOT address frontier model development — designed to complement
    SB-53 and the RAISE Act, not replace them

  Resilient Benefits (addresses the "rapidly changing tech" scoring criterion):
  • Audit standards are set by AIAOB rulemaking, not locked into statute — they
    evolve as AI capabilities change without requiring new legislation
  • Change-triggered audits mean no system can be certified and then materially
    altered without review
  • Three reasons this survives a deregulatory environment:
    (1) Market-creating — builds a new professional industry, not just costs
    (2) Fixes market failure — companies cannot credibly self-audit; investors and
        customers cannot verify safety claims without an independent standard
    (3) Reduces litigation risk — a defined audit standard limits open-ended
        liability; compliant companies have a safe harbor industry should want

  What to write: Lead with benefits, then name the trade-offs honestly — judges
  score on whether you address them, not whether they exist. The three "deregulatory
  survival" arguments are your closing move; they reframe the policy from "regulation"
  to "market infrastructure." End on resilience — it's a scoring criterion and it's
  your strongest closing argument.

Q&A PREP  (not in the brief — for presentation only)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Isn't this too burdensome for small startups?
A: The trigger thresholds (>10K decisions/year, named high-risk sectors) exempt small
   or low-risk deployments entirely. A recipe recommender isn't audited. A loan
   underwriting tool sold to banks is.

Q: Why not just use FTC or NIST?
A: FTC is overloaded with existing enforcement. NIST has no enforcement authority —
   its AI Risk Management Framework is voluntary. The PCAOB model works because it is
   independent, self-funding, and has real teeth.

Q: Republicans want to abolish PCAOB — why model on something under threat?
A: The PCAOB debate is about accounting industry consolidation, not the oversight
   model. The AIAOB is market-creating — it builds a new professional industry —
   which gives it bipartisan appeal independent of the PCAOB's political fate.

Q: What if an AI system qualifies for multiple tiers?
A: The highest applicable tier governs. A healthcare hiring tool (Tier 2 for hiring,
   Tier 1 for healthcare) gets Tier 1 treatment.

Q: How do you certify enough auditors fast enough?
A: Phase in by tier. Tier 1 is the smallest set and highest stakes — stand it up
   first. Existing cybersecurity and financial compliance auditors are a natural
   pipeline. AIAOB sets provisional standards in year 1, full standards by year 2.
